ExoHunter bridges astronomy and artificial intelligence — transforming how we identify worlds beyond our Solar System. This page will guide you through the essential concepts that make our mission possible.

What Are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars beyond our Solar System. Thousands have been discovered using data from NASA’s missions like Kepler, K2, and TESS. Each detection brings us closer to understanding how common — or rare — Earth-like worlds are.

stars

Why Search for Exoplanets?

Finding exoplanets helps us answer fundamental questions about our place in the universe. Are there other habitable worlds? How do planetary systems form and evolve? Each new discovery expands our knowledge of the cosmos and the potential for life beyond Earth.

numbers

How Many Exoplanets Are There?

As of now, thousands of exoplanets have been confirmed, with many more candidates awaiting validation. The exact number is constantly changing as new discoveries are made and old data is reanalyzed. See NASA's Exoplanet Archive to explore the latest findings.

NASA Missions

Kepler and TESS are space telescopes designed to find exoplanets by monitoring the brightness of stars. They collect vast amounts of data, capturing light curves that reveal potential planetary transits.

data_usage

What Are Light Curves?

Light curves are graphs that show how a star’s brightness changes over time. By analyzing these curves, astronomers can identify potential exoplanets.

light_mode

The Transit Method

This is the most successful technique for detecting exoplanets. When a planet passes in front of its star, it slightly dims the star’s light. By studying these periodic dips, we can infer a planet’s size, orbit, and even atmospheric hints.

How AI Helps

Instead of manually analyzing thousands of light curves, we use machine learning models that automatically detect subtle patterns distinguishing exoplanets from false positives.

science

The ExoHunter Model

Our model uses data from NASA missions to train models such as LightGBM and 1D Convolutional Neural Networks. These algorithms learn to recognize transit signals and classify new candidates with high accuracy.

Why It Matters

Each detection helps astronomers prioritize telescope time, refine planetary catalogs, and expand humanity's search for habitable worlds. With AI, we accelerate discovery — one data point at a time.
